What it is

This site tracks trends in the published research literature on linguistics, applied linguistics, and language education, and records how those trends change over time. It refreshes automatically every week. Its main purpose is the long game: by storing a dated snapshot every week, it builds a permanent record so that, months and years from now, you can see how the field actually shifted.

Where the data comes from

All counts come from OpenAlex, an open index of scholarly works whose metadata is released under a CC0 (public-domain) licence. The corpus is the union of the two OpenAlex subfields that hold linguistics: Language and Linguistics (in Arts & Humanities) and Linguistics and Language (in Social Sciences). Both are included on purpose: the Arts & Humanities branch is several times larger, and using only one would silently drop most of the literature, including much of sociolinguistics, pragmatics, and language policy.

How trends are measured

  • Share, not raw counts. Each topic is measured as its share of all linguistics output in a rolling 12-month window. The index itself grows every year, so a rising raw count is often just more indexing; share is what stays comparable across years.
  • Year-over-year. The headline movement compares the most recent 12 months against the 12 months before that.
  • Guards against false trends. A topic must clear a minimum volume and must keep moving the same direction across several consecutive snapshots before it is shown as a confirmed trend. Anything younger is marked early.
  • Provisional recent data. The most recent weeks are always incomplete, because papers keep being indexed for weeks or months after publication. Early in the site's life the whole picture is labelled as still building a baseline.
  • Stable identifiers. Trends are tracked on OpenAlex's stable topic IDs, and the taxonomy is snapshotted each run, so reclassification by OpenAlex is detectable rather than silently corrupting a trend line.

What it covers, and what it misses

This is an observatory of the indexed, mostly-English, journal-article slice of the field. Be honest with yourself about the blind spots:

  • Books and edited volumes are under-represented, and they carry real theoretical weight in this field.
  • Non-English scholarship (for example Francophone, Hispanophone, and Sinophone work) is under-indexed.
  • Conference proceedings and grey literature are patchy; the computational-language slice lives largely on preprint servers and is only partially captured here.
  • A meaningful share of records lack abstracts, and language labels are imperfect.

So read these as trends in the indexed journal literature, not a complete picture of the field. That framing is honest and defensible; an over-claim of completeness would not be.

Papers and licensing

The paper lists show titles, authors, venues, and links only. The site does not store or redisplay abstract text; where a free full-text version exists, the link points to it.

How it runs

A small scheduled job queries OpenAlex once a week, writes a dated snapshot into a public repository, and rebuilds these pages. The repository's own history is the longitudinal archive. There is no server and no database to maintain.
